Limits and Continuity

The foundation of everything in calculus. Master how a function behaves as the input approaches a value — and what it means for that function to be unbroken across its domain.

1 Definition of a Limit

A limit describes the value a function $f(x)$ approaches as $x$ gets arbitrarily close to some point $a$. We write this as

$$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L $$

Intuitively, $L$ is the "target" of $f(x)$ — even if $f(a)$ itself is undefined, weird, or simply different from $L$. Many limits can be evaluated by direct substitution, but the truly interesting ones happen where direct substitution fails and the function's behavior near the point reveals something the value at the point cannot.

Formal (ε–δ) Definition

The precise version, used in proofs: $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means that for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that whenever $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$.

𝓲
Note. The condition $0 < |x - a|$ explicitly excludes $x = a$. The limit asks only about behavior near $a$, not at $a$ itself.

2 Evaluating Limits

Different functions call for different techniques. The table below maps common function shapes to the method that resolves them.

Function Type Method Example
Polynomial Direct substitution $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 2}(x^{2}+3x) = 10$
Rational, defined at $a$ Direct substitution $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1}\frac{x+1}{x-3} = -1$
Rational, $\tfrac{0}{0}$ form Factor & cancel $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 2}\frac{x^{2}-4}{x-2} = 4$
Radical expression Multiply by conjugate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x+1}-1}{x} = \tfrac{1}{2}$
Trigonometric Standard limits / L'Hôpital $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$
Exponential / log L'Hôpital or rewrite $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{e^{x}-1}{x} = 1$

3 One-Sided Limits

Sometimes a function approaches different values from the left and from the right. We capture this with one-sided limits:

$$ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) \quad\text{and}\quad \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) $$

The two-sided limit $\lim\limits_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and agree. If the left and right limits disagree (as in a jump discontinuity), the two-sided limit does not exist.

4 Continuity

A function $f$ is continuous at $x = a$ precisely when all three of the following hold:

  1. $f(a)$ is defined.
  2. $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.

Break any of these and you have a discontinuity. The three classical flavors are:

Type What fails Removable?
Removable (hole) $f(a)$ undefined or mismatched Yes — redefine $f(a)$
Jump Left and right limits differ No
Infinite Function grows without bound near $a$ No
★
Intermediate Value Theorem. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $N$ lies between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists at least one $c \in (a,b)$ with $f(c) = N$. This is why continuous functions hit every value in between — no skipping allowed.

5 Limit-Evaluation Techniques

A working list of the moves you will use over and over. Reach for them in roughly this order:

  • Direct substitution. Plug in $a$. If the result is finite, you are done.
  • Factor and cancel. Best for $\tfrac{0}{0}$ in rational expressions.
  • Multiply by the conjugate. Removes square roots that block cancellation.
  • Common denominator. Cleans up nested fractions before evaluating.
  • Trig identities. Use $\sin^{2}x + \cos^{2}x = 1$ and the half-angle / double-angle formulas to rewrite stuck expressions.
  • L'Hôpital's Rule. When the form is $\tfrac{0}{0}$ or $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, differentiate top and bottom separately and try again.
  • Squeeze Theorem. If $g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)$ near $a$ and $\lim g = \lim h = L$, then $\lim f = L$.
Indeterminate Forms to Recognize

The seven canonical indeterminate forms — they look like algebra but hide real behavior — are $\tfrac{0}{0}$, $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, $0 \cdot \infty$, $\infty - \infty$, $0^{0}$, $1^{\infty}$, and $\infty^{0}$. Each one can be reduced to $\tfrac{0}{0}$ or $\tfrac{\infty}{\infty}$ with the right algebraic move, opening the door for L'Hôpital's Rule.
